3.1 Key Performance Indicators

Key performance indicators (KPIs) are the most important variables predicting performance. These differ from sport to sport, and not all are contuitive. At the SportsMechanist we’ve selected the most reliable KPIs based on scientific literature and biomechanical reports from World Championships.
We have divided the KPIs into groups of ‘linear’ and ‘angular’. Linear KPIs provide information about linear distances and velocities, as well as time. Angular KPIs on the other hand provide information about angles and the velocity at which the change. These are in turn relevant in explaining how what the movements look like.

A note on contact times: Contact times are often reported, however they are a double edged sword. Shorter contact times are correlated with longer jumps, however the relationship is not as straightforward as that. Contact times in the triple jump is mainly thought to be a consequence of horizontal velocity. The faster the velocity, the shorter time the jumper has on the ground before having to take-off again. So what seems a correlation between contact times and result is in fact correlation between horizontal velocity and result. Indeed, it is the target for the jumper to exert the greatest amount of force (impulse) on the ground, and following the impulse equation \(F*t=m*v\) either increasing the amount of force OR time will increase the velocity of the jumper. As noted by [19], the consequence is that shorter contact times alone are not desirable.

Angular KPIs are concerned with angles; angles of various joints, but also others, e.g. the take-off angle. These metrics provide great information about the generation and nature of the movements themselves, not just their outcomes.
